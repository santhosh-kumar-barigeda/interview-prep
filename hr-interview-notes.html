Chapter 4

Behavioural Questions (STAR)

STAR Method: Structure every behavioural answer as Situation → Task → Action → Result. Keep it concise — 2 minutes max per answer.

Teamwork & Collaboration

Q: Tell me about a time you worked effectively in a team.
S: At Whiztek, we were building MyBibleIQ with a small cross-functional team — myself on mobile, a backend developer, and a designer.
T: We needed to ship the multiplayer feature within a tight deadline while the backend APIs were still being defined.
A: I proactively coordinated daily syncs, mocked the API contracts on my side so I could build in parallel, and flagged blockers early so the backend dev could prioritise.
R: We shipped the feature on time and the parallel workflow became our standard practice for future features.
Q: Describe a situation where you had a conflict with a colleague. How did you resolve it?
S: There was a disagreement with the backend developer on how data should be structured for the leaderboard API — their approach would have required complex client-side transformations on every render.
T: I needed to advocate for a better contract without creating friction.
A: I put together a quick comparison showing the performance cost of each approach and brought it to a sync rather than pushing back directly.
R: We landed on a revised structure that worked for both sides, and it became a habit for us to align on API contracts early.
Q: Tell me about a time you helped a teammate who was struggling.
S: A junior developer who joined the project was struggling to understand our state management setup with Zustand and TanStack Query.
T: I needed to get them productive quickly without slowing down my own work.
A: I scheduled a focused 45-minute walkthrough, created a short internal doc explaining the patterns we used, and reviewed their first few PRs closely with detailed comments.
R: Within two weeks they were shipping features independently and the doc became a reference for anyone new joining the project.

Problem Solving & Pressure

Q: Tell me about a challenging problem you solved.
S: Our app bundle had grown to 50MB which was causing install drop-offs and slow startup times.
T: I needed to reduce size without breaking any functionality.
A: I audited every dependency, removed unused packages, replaced heavy ones with lighter alternatives, optimised all image assets, and split code where possible.
R: Bundle size dropped from 50MB to 38MB — a 24% reduction — and startup time improved measurably.
Q: Describe a time you worked under pressure or a tight deadline.
S: We had a client demo scheduled and three screens were incomplete two days before.
T: I had to ship functional, polished screens without compromising quality.
A: I broke the work into the smallest possible tasks, focused on what was visible in the demo flow, and deferred non-visible polish to after the demo. I communicated the scope clearly to the client before the demo.
R: The demo went well, the client approved the direction, and the remaining polish was shipped within the following week.
Q: Tell me about a time you had to learn something quickly.
S: We needed to implement high-performance SVG path animations for a gamified progress screen. I had never used React Native Skia before.
T: The feature was on the critical path with a one-week window.
A: I spent two focused days going through the Skia docs and examples, built isolated prototypes to understand the rendering model, then integrated it into the feature.
R: The animation shipped on schedule and became one of the most visually distinctive parts of the app.

Leadership & Initiative

Q: Tell me about a time you took initiative without being asked.
S: We had no automated build or release process — every release was manual and error-prone.
T: This wasn't on the roadmap but it was costing us time every cycle.
A: I set up Fastlane and GitHub Actions pipelines for both iOS and Android on my own time, including automated versioning, signing, and staged rollout support.
R: Release time dropped significantly and we eliminated manual signing errors entirely. It was then adopted as our standard delivery process.
Q: Describe a time you led a project or a team.
S: From day one at Whiztek I was the sole mobile developer on MyBibleIQ.
T: I had to make all architectural decisions, manage feature delivery, and coordinate with the backend team and client — while building 80+ screens.
A: I established a feature-based folder structure, defined API contracts collaboratively, ran weekly client demos, and owned the full release pipeline.
R: The app shipped to production on schedule with a scalable codebase that supports 10+ modules today.
Q: Tell me about a time you influenced someone to change their mind.
S: The client initially wanted to use Redux for state management because it was familiar to them.
T: I believed Zustand was a better fit for our app's scale and team size.
A: I prepared a short comparison showing bundle size impact, boilerplate overhead, and migration cost for our specific use case. I presented it during a planning call with clear trade-offs rather than just pushing my preference.
R: The client agreed to go with Zustand and it's been a solid choice — the store layer is clean, lightweight, and easy to extend.

Adaptability & Change

Q: Tell me about a time you adapted to a major change at work.
S: Midway through the project, the client shifted a significant part of the product scope — a planned offline-first feature was deprioritised and replaced with a real-time multiplayer system, which was far more complex.
T: I had to pivot the architecture I'd planned and learn WebSocket-based real-time patterns quickly.
A: I re-evaluated the state management approach, researched real-time patterns in React Native, and rebuilt the affected module with the new scope.
R: The multiplayer feature shipped and is now one of the core engagement drivers in the app.
Q: Describe a time you received critical feedback. How did you respond?
S: After a client demo, I received feedback that the onboarding flow felt cluttered and confusing, even though I'd followed the original design spec closely.
T: I had to separate my own attachment to what I'd built and genuinely understand the user concern.
A: I went back, reviewed the flow with fresh eyes, simplified the screen count, and improved the visual hierarchy. I also started validating UI decisions with the designer earlier in the process.
R: The revised flow was approved in the very next demo and the client called it a significant improvement.
Q: Tell me about a time priorities shifted mid-project. What did you do?
S: We were mid-sprint on the social features when a critical performance issue was reported on the quiz module affecting mid-range devices.
T: I had to pause planned work and triage an unplanned but urgent issue.
A: I used the React Native DevTools profiler to identify the rendering bottleneck — a large FlatList with unoptimised item components. I applied memoisation and optimised the list rendering, then communicated the revised sprint plan to the client.
R: The issue was resolved within a day, performance was restored, and we adjusted the sprint timeline with minimal impact to the overall delivery.

Work Style & Culture Fit

Work Preferences

Q: Do you prefer working independently or in a team?
Both, depending on the context. I'm comfortable working independently — I've led an entire mobile product solo — but I genuinely enjoy collaborating with a team. Code reviews, architecture discussions, and a second set of eyes on hard problems make the output better. I thrive in environments where there's a good mix of autonomy and collaboration.
Q: How do you prioritise tasks when you have multiple deadlines?
I prioritise by impact and dependency. I ask: what is blocking someone else, what is client-visible, and what has the highest risk if delayed. I use a simple written list to track in-progress tasks and flag when something needs renegotiating. I'd rather be transparent about what's slipping than silently miss a deadline.
Q: How do you handle working on multiple projects simultaneously?
I time-box and context-switch deliberately rather than juggling everything at once. I block focus time per project, keep notes on where I left off to reduce ramp-up time, and communicate timelines proactively so no one is surprised by delays. At Whiztek I managed both the mobile app and the Next.js web version in parallel using this approach.
Q: What kind of work environment do you thrive in?
I thrive in environments with high ownership, clear goals, and a strong engineering culture. I like when teams care about quality — code reviews, good architecture decisions, and clean delivery — but also move fast. I've worked fully remote for 2+ years and am comfortable with async communication and self-direction.

Communication & Feedback

Q: How do you communicate progress to your team or manager?
I provide regular updates without waiting to be asked. At Whiztek I ran weekly client demos to showcase progress and used async messages to flag blockers the moment they appeared. I believe bad news shared early is always better than bad news shared late — it gives the team time to adapt.
Q: How do you handle disagreements with your manager?
I voice my perspective clearly and back it up with reasoning — not just opinion. If I disagree on a technical decision I'll put together a quick comparison or example to make the discussion concrete. But once a decision is made I commit to it fully. I'd rather disagree and commit than silently resist.
Q: How do you give and receive feedback?
I give feedback that's specific and actionable — I focus on the decision or the code, not the person. When receiving feedback I try to separate my ego from my work. If someone points out a better way to do something, that's a good thing. I've learned some of my best practices from code review comments.

Chapter 7

Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Tailor your questions to who you're speaking with. Ask a recruiter about process and culture, ask engineers about the tech stack and day-to-day, ask a hiring manager about expectations and growth. Always prepare 3–5 questions per round.

HR / Recruiter Screening

You're speaking with HR or a talent partner. Focus on the process, the role overview, and logistics.

  • Can you walk me through the interview process and how many rounds are there?
  • What is the expected timeline for making a hiring decision?
  • What does the team structure look like for this role?
  • What are the most important qualities you're looking for in this hire?
  • Is this a new position or a backfill?

Technical Round 1 (DSA / Coding)

You're speaking with an engineer. Focus on the tech stack, coding practices, and day-to-day work.

  • What does the tech stack look like for this team?
  • How does the team approach code reviews?
  • What does a typical sprint or iteration look like?
  • What kind of problems will I be solving day-to-day?
  • How much ownership do engineers have over their features end-to-end?

Technical Round 2 (System Design / Deep Dive)

Typically a senior engineer or tech lead. Go deeper on architecture, scale, and engineering culture.

  • What are the biggest technical challenges the team is currently facing?
  • How does the team handle technical debt?
  • How are architectural or technology decisions made on the team?
  • What does the on-call or incident response process look like?
  • What does the testing and deployment pipeline look like?

Hiring Manager Round

You're speaking with the direct manager. Focus on expectations, team dynamics, growth, and your fit.

  • What does success look like in this role after 3 months and after 1 year?
  • What are the biggest challenges the person in this role would face early on?
  • How do you support the growth and development of your team members?
  • How does the team handle disagreements or differing technical opinions?
  • What do you enjoy most about working here?

Final / HR Closure Round

Often a final culture-fit or offer discussion with HR. Focus on logistics, compensation, and next steps.

  • What does the onboarding process look like for new engineers?
  • What learning and development opportunities does the company offer?
  • Is there anything from our conversations today that gives you pause about my fit for this role?
  • What are the next steps and when can I expect to hear back?
